All of you have seen what an astrological chart looks like.  However, do you know
what you’re actually looking at?  Do you understand the physical phenomena of the
chart structure?  Are you familiar with the mathematical formulas utilized in chart
calculation and construction?  Since the advent of astrology software and free sites
on the internet, chart calculation has become as simple as the click of a mouse.
Unfortunately this easy access has robbed many astrology students of an experience
that offers a more fundamental grasp of the very tools they are using.
In addition, the use of astrological software, internet produced charts and related
material has replaced the use of reference books for many students, such as an
Ephemeris or Table of Houses.  While astrological software is an essential time
saving tool for the modern astrologer, the student misses out on the very useful
process of learning how to keep track of the planets in an Ephemeris.  Not only is an
Ephemeris a practical tool for keeping-up with day to day, or even monthly solar,
lunar or planetary positions, but the simple turning of pages in a 50 year or 100 year
Ephemeris provides an immediate reference to long term planetary cycles.  You can
look back to see what happened the last time a similar planetary position occurred
and you can look forward to see when it will occur again.

DECLINATION – means north or south of the celestial equator.  If 2 planets share the same exact
degree north or south in declination they are said to make an aspect called a parallel – this aspect
is listed in your aspectarian as II, or if one planet is the same degree north and the other is the
same degree south they are called contraparallel listed in your aspectarian as II.  The parallel is
treated like a conjunction, the contraparallel like an opposition, both with an orb of only 1 degree.
Not all astrologers use declination, the consensus is that these aspects reinforce major aspects.

LATITUDE in an Ephemeris – means north or south of the ecliptic, called ‘planetary latitude.’
Planetary latitude is rarely used or even understood by most astrologers.  The degrees north or
south of the ecliptic are relatively small compared to declination.  However, some financial
astrologers have found latitude to correlate with both the commodity market and stock market
price action.  Latitude deserves further research in regards to natal chart analysis and forecasting.

KOCH Tables of Houses
Both copies of the Birthplace Tables of Houses are nearly identical on the inside, the difference

The Koch Tables of Houses, as they are commonly known, are the 2nd most popular book of
Houses.  The most common book of houses is the Placidus House System sometimes called
Dalton’s Placidus Tables of Houses.  Placidus is the most common in part due to it being the first
Table of House available for use in the west for nearly 70 years.  The Koch Tables of House were
developed by Walter A. Koch, a German astrologer that worked on perfecting these tables for
some thirty years, including those years he spent in a Nazi concentration camp during World War II
– during which his books and tables were confiscated and destroyed.  Having the Sun, Moon and
ASCN in Virgo, he continued to work on these tables in his mind and when WWII ended he
published his work and wrote articles justifying his techniques and methodology.  The Koch Tables
first became popular in the United States during the early 1970’s and have nearly surpassed the
Placidus Tables in usage at this point in western astrology.  Both the Placidus House Tables and the
Koch House Tables list the same Ascendant and Mid-heaven, where they differ is on the cusps of
the ‘intermediate houses,’ the 11th House, the 12th House, the 2nd House and the 3rd House cusps,
all of which are listed in both books.  There are quite a number of other Tables of Houses in use.
Perhaps the most common being the Equal House System, which measures all of the houses in
equal 30 degree segments starting with the Ascendant.

All house systems are attempts to divide the celestial sphere above into sections. Most house
systems utilize formulas for dividing the sky above and below the horizon.  The Whole Sign system
simply designates an entire House as the ‘whole sign’ for whatever sign is on the House cusp,
regardless of the degree; an ASCN of 29 degrees of Leo would still make the entire 1st House Leo
regardless.  Other House Systems are more complex and make use of different coordinates
involving the celestial sphere.  Much of the mathematical conflict regarding house systems and
tables is a dispute over how to “tri-sect the diurnal arc,” which means simply, how to divide the
sky between sunrise and true noon into 3 sections. This issue is hotly debated.  So far no one has
proven their house system to be any better than that of Walter A. Koch.  However, the Equal
House System is recommended for far northern latitudes, especially above 55 degrees of latitude,
where all house systems begin to break down due to the curvature of the earth.  Remember, at
the North Pole the Sun is actually below the horizon for six months!  This renders all other house
systems irrelevant at such extreme latitudes, other than equal house.  However, it is important to
remember that western astrology was developed mainly in temperate zone regions and some
northern tropic areas of latitude, in other words in those areas of terrestrial latitude where most
people live.  Thus these house systems evolved in accordance with human life in the most
populated and livable regions of our planet.

Step 1: Calculating Local Mean Time
Local Mean Time (LMT), sometimes called True Local Time (TLT), is a ‘mean’
approximation of sundial time, used locally for thousands of years.  Prior to 1883 time was
a local matter.  Even with the advent of mechanical clocks during the Renaissance in
Europe, every village and town had a clock tower in the center of town that showed the
time for that place.  The clock time in villages only ninety miles apart would be different,
just as the shadows of an accurate sundial would vary slightly from place to place.
The increased speed of railway transportation and even maritime shipping necessitated
reliance upon a more uniform or standardized way of measuring time.  Changing the time
on watches aboard a train as it passed through every town and hamlet, and the resulting
clutter of scheduled arrival and departure times brought about the use of a uniform time
called Railway Time.  Railway Time was applied to regions divided by lines of longitude on a
map.  These regions were sectioned into areas called time zones.  Reliance upon Railway
Time would result in the World Time Zone convention that met in Greenwich, England and
agreed on Nov. 18, 1883 to set the boundaries of time zones internationally around the
globe.  A time zone was established for each 15 degrees of longitude from 0 degrees of
longitude.  Any zone east of 0 degrees would be a + zone and any time zone west of 0
degrees would be a – zone.  Each time zone would be defined by what is called the
Standard Meridian.  The Standard Meridian is the line of longitude along which the time
observed coincide exactly with Local Mean Time.  For example, the Standard Meridian for
Beijing Time or Zone + 8 is 120◦E 00.’ You can always find the Standard Meridian for any
time zone by multiplying the zone number by 15.
Zone + 8 X 15 = 120 degrees.  All time zones are 15 degrees of longitude apart.  This is
because the Sun appears to traverse 1 hour or 60 minutes of time for every 15 degrees of
longitude, (Of course it is the earth’s rotation we’re actually measuring.)  This ratio of 15
degrees of longitude = 1 hour can be reduced further:
15 degrees of longitude  = 1 hour or 60 minutes of time
60 minutes ÷ 15 degrees of longitude = 4 minutes of time
Thus, 1 degree of longitude = 4 minutes of time
This ratio of 1 degree of longitude corresponding to 4 minutes of time is very important!
We will use this equivalency when using longitude to covert from Standard Time back to
Local Mean Time in order to establish the time closest to the actual sundial time of the
locale of the chart in question.  This correspondence can be further reduced to 4 seconds
of time being = to 1 minute of longitude.

Further Calculation Considerations:
1). When calculating Sidereal Time for a birthplace with Western Longitude you must add the
fourth step instead of subtracting it from your sidereal time formula.  The fourth step is the step
that corrects 2 seconds X each 3 degrees of Longitude from 0 degrees Longitude.
If the birth Longitude was 120W00, you would ÷ 120 by 3 = 40 X 2 seconds = 80 seconds, or 1
minute and 20 seconds.  You would then ADD this amount as the fourth and final step to your
sidereal time formula for your Calculated Sidereal Time at birth.
This correction of 2 seconds for each 3 degrees of Longitude, corrects for the ‘actual time of the
earth’s rotation’ when compared to the artificially imposed measurement of Longitude.  Nature,
nor our solar system, fits ‘exactly’ the lines we impose as measurements.  Likewise the Earth is not
a perfect sphere.  The formula of 4 minutes of time for each degree of longitude is a good formula
for measurement except that for every 3 degrees of Longitude we lose 2 seconds of time.
2.) When calculating Sidereal Time for a birthplace with Southern Latitude the MC or M remains
the same as for Northern Latitude.  However to find the correct Ascendant and house cusps you
must follow these 3 steps:
1. Find the opposition point of the MC:
2. Refer to your Tables of Houses for Northern Latitude to find the corresponding Ascendant for
this opposite MC.
3. Now, once again, take the opposition point, to this Ascendant and that will be your Ascendant
at Southern Latitudes, and the same for all of the other house cusps listed in your Tables of Houses.
Example:
Suppose the calculated MC was 17 degrees Libra 00’
The opposition point would be 17 degrees Aries 00’
Look up 17 degrees Aries in your Tables of Houses for the corresponding Ascendant in the latitude
column.  For latitude of 24 degrees this would be 23 degrees Cancer 49.’
The opposition point for 23 degrees Cancer 49’ is 23 degrees Capricorn 49.’
So your Ascendant for 24S00’ latitude for a MC of 17 degrees Libra is 23 deg. Capricorn 49.’

Daylight Savings Time
Whenever Daylight Savings Time is in effect it will add one additional step to your calculation of
Local Mean Time, and make a one hour difference in your calculation of Universal Time (UT).
1. When calculating LMT for daylight savings time you must subtract one hour first from the
standard time of birth.
If the person was born at 14:37 Beijing Daylight Savings Time, that means the clock had
been moved forward one hour, thus to change it back to standard Beijing Time you would
subtract one hour and your standard Beijing Time would be 13:37.
2. When calculating UT for daylight savings time you must realize that for Beijing time you are
now 9 hours ahead rather than 8 hours ahead.  If a person was born at 8:00 Beijing
Daylight Savings Time on July 1.  This would be 23:00 UT June 30.
